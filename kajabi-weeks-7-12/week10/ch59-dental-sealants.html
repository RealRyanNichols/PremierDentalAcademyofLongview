Dental sealants are a thin, protective coating applied to the pits and fissures of teeth to prevent decay. The deep grooves of posterior teeth trap food and plaque and are difficult to clean with a toothbrush, making them the most common site for caries to begin. Sealants physically block these areas. This lesson explains how sealants work, which teeth and patients benefit most, the materials used, and the step-by-step technique with an emphasis on a clean, dry, well-isolated tooth.

Learning objectives

  • Explain the purpose of dental sealants and how they prevent caries.
  • Identify the teeth and patients most likely to benefit from sealants.
  • Compare the main sealant materials and their indications.

  • Explain why isolation and a dry field are critical to sealant success.
  • Describe how to evaluate and maintain sealants over time.

How sealants prevent decay

The chewing surfaces of premolars and molars have deep pits and fissures that a toothbrush bristle cannot reach. Bacteria and fermentable carbohydrates collect there, producing acid that demineralizes enamel and starts a cavity. A sealant flows into and bonds to these grooves, forming a physical barrier that keeps bacteria and food out. Sealants address pit-and-fissure caries; they complement, but do not replace, fluoride, which protects the smooth surfaces.

Who and which teeth benefit

  • Children and adolescents benefit most, ideally soon after a permanent molar erupts and before decay begins.
  • Newly erupted permanent first and second molars are the most common teeth sealed; premolars and primary molars may also be sealed when indicated.
  • Sealants are placed on sound (caries-free) or very early non-cavitated surfaces, not on teeth with existing cavitated decay that requires a restoration.

Sealant materials

  • Resin-based sealants are the most widely used. They are cured (hardened) either by a curing light (light-cured) or chemically (self-cured), and they bond to acid-etched enamel. They generally provide strong retention when the field is kept dry.
  • Glass ionomer sealants release fluoride and are more tolerant of moisture, which can make them useful when ideal isolation is difficult, such as on a partially erupted molar. They tend to be less wear-resistant than resin sealants.

Application technique

Sealant success depends on meticulous technique. Moisture or contamination of the etched surface is the leading cause of sealant failure, so isolation and a dry field are essential throughout.

  1. Clean the tooth. Remove plaque and debris from the occlusal surface so the sealant contacts clean enamel.
  2. Isolate the tooth. Use a dental dam or cotton rolls with a dry-field system; effective isolation keeps saliva off the tooth.
  3. Etch the enamel. Apply etchant (typically phosphoric acid) to the pits and fissures for the manufacturer-specified time to create a microscopically roughened surface for bonding.
  4. Rinse and dry. Thoroughly rinse the etchant and dry; properly etched enamel appears frosty or chalky white. If it is contaminated by saliva, re-etch.
  5. Apply the sealant. Flow the material into the pits and fissures, avoiding bubbles and overfilling.
  6. Cure. Light-cure for the recommended time (using protective eyewear/shielding) or allow a self-cured material to set.
  7. Evaluate. Check that the sealant is hardened and fully covers the grooves; inspect for voids.
  8. Check occlusion. Verify the bite with articulating paper and adjust any high spots so the patient bites evenly.

Evaluation and maintenance

Sealants are checked at recall visits for retention and integrity. A sealant that is partially lost or worn can be repaired or replaced. Because sealants can be lost over time, ongoing monitoring is part of the preventive program; sealed teeth still need regular brushing, flossing, and fluoride.

Key terms

  • Pit and fissure — the grooves and depressions on the chewing surfaces of posterior teeth.
  • Sealant — a protective coating that fills and seals pits and fissures to prevent decay.
  • Etchant — an acid (commonly phosphoric acid) that roughens enamel to improve bonding.
  • Isolation — keeping the tooth dry and free of saliva during treatment.
  • Resin-based sealant — the most common sealant type, cured by light or chemically.
  • Glass ionomer sealant — a fluoride-releasing, moisture-tolerant sealant material.
  • Curing — the hardening of the sealant material.

Chairside notes

Chairside tip: A dry tooth is everything. Keep the operating field isolated from the moment you etch until the sealant is cured, and if saliva touches the etched surface at any point, rinse, re-isolate, and re-etch rather than placing sealant over a contaminated tooth. After curing, run the explorer over the surface to confirm it is fully set and void-free, then check and adjust the bite. Confirm with your supervising dentist whether placing sealants is within your legal scope and what supervision is required.

Summary

Dental sealants prevent pit-and-fissure caries by sealing the deep grooves of posterior teeth where brushing cannot reach. They are most effective on newly erupted, caries-free permanent molars in children and adolescents and complement fluoride. Resin-based sealants are most common and require a dry, acid-etched surface, while glass ionomer materials tolerate moisture and release fluoride. Careful isolation, proper etching, complete coverage, full curing, and a bite check are the keys to a successful, long-lasting sealant. Always confirm state-specific scope and supervision before placing sealants.
