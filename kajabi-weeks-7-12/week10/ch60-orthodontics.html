Orthodontics is the dental specialty concerned with the diagnosis, prevention, and correction of malocclusion (improper bite) and irregularities of the teeth and jaws. Straight teeth and a healthy bite improve function, make oral hygiene easier, and support self-confidence. As a dental assistant in an orthodontic setting, you help with records, support the placement and adjustment of appliances, and teach patients how to care for their teeth and appliances. This lesson covers the causes and classification of malocclusion, orthodontic records, appliances and components, and the assistant's role.

Learning objectives

  • Define orthodontics and malocclusion and explain why orthodontic treatment matters.
  • Describe the causes of malocclusion and Angle's classification of occlusion.
  • Identify the diagnostic records used in orthodontics.
  • Recognize the components of fixed appliances and common removable appliances.
  • Describe oral hygiene and dietary guidance for orthodontic patients.
  • Explain the dental assistant's role in supporting orthodontic treatment.

Malocclusion and its causes

Occlusion is the way the maxillary and mandibular teeth contact when the jaws close; malocclusion is any deviation from normal occlusion. Causes are generally grouped as:

  • Genetic/inherited — tooth size, jaw size, and skeletal patterns passed through families.
  • Environmental/acquired — habits such as prolonged thumb sucking or tongue thrusting, premature loss of primary teeth, and trauma.

Angle's classification

Edward Angle's classification describes the relationship of the maxillary and mandibular first molars:

  • Class I — normal molar relationship; the bite is correct front-to-back even though individual teeth may be crowded or rotated.
  • Class II — the mandible (lower jaw) is positioned distally (back) relative to the maxilla; often appears as an "overbite" with retruded lower jaw.
  • Class III — the mandible is positioned mesially (forward) relative to the maxilla; often appears as an "underbite."

Other terms describe specific relationships, such as overjet (horizontal projection of the upper front teeth over the lower) and overbite (vertical overlap of the front teeth), as well as crossbite, open bite, and crowding.

Orthodontic records

Diagnosis and treatment planning rely on a complete set of records:

  • Medical and dental history and clinical examination.
  • Radiographs, including a panoramic film and a cephalometric (ceph) radiograph used to analyze skeletal and dental relationships. Specific radiographic exposure tasks are governed by state law. [VERIFY: Amanda] (Texas requirements for who may expose orthodontic radiographs.)
  • Photographs of the face and teeth.
  • Diagnostic models (study casts) made from impressions or intraoral scans.

Appliances and components

Fixed appliances

Fixed appliances (braces) are bonded to the teeth and stay in place throughout treatment. Common components include:

  • Bands — metal rings cemented around teeth, often molars, to anchor the appliance.
  • Brackets — small attachments bonded to the teeth that hold the archwire.
  • Archwire — the wire that runs through the brackets and applies controlled force to move teeth.
  • Ligatures — small ties (elastic or wire) that hold the archwire in the bracket.
  • Elastics (rubber bands) — apply additional force between arches; the patient often changes these.
  • Separators (spacers) — placed between teeth to create space before bands are seated.

Removable appliances

  • Aligners — a series of clear, removable trays that gradually move teeth.
  • Retainers — worn after active treatment to hold teeth in their new positions during the retention phase. Retention is essential because teeth tend to relapse toward their original positions.
  • Headgear and other functional/space-related appliances may be used in specific cases.

Patient care and oral hygiene

Orthodontic appliances trap food and plaque, raising the risk of decay and white-spot decalcification, gingivitis, and staining. Patient education is a major part of orthodontic assisting:

  • Brush thoroughly after meals and at bedtime, cleaning around brackets and at the gumline.
  • Use aids such as interdental brushes, floss threaders, or a water flosser to clean under the archwire.
  • Use fluoride as recommended to protect against white-spot lesions.
  • Avoid hard, sticky, and chewy foods that can break or dislodge appliances.

Patients may have soreness after adjustments; reassure them this is normal and usually temporary, and review what to do if a wire or bracket comes loose.

Key terms

  • Malocclusion — any deviation from normal occlusion.
  • Angle's classification — a system describing molar relationships (Class I, II, III).
  • Overjet — horizontal projection of upper anterior teeth over the lower.
  • Overbite — vertical overlap of the anterior teeth.
  • Bracket — an attachment bonded to a tooth that holds the archwire.
  • Archwire — the wire that applies force to move teeth.
  • Separator (spacer) — a device that creates space before band placement.
  • Cephalometric radiograph — a film used to analyze skeletal and dental relationships.
  • Retention — the phase of holding teeth in their new positions after active treatment.

Chairside notes

Chairside tip: Hygiene coaching is one of the highest-value things you do in an ortho practice. Use a disclosing aid and a model to show patients exactly how to clean around brackets and under the wire, and reinforce avoiding hard and sticky foods that break appliances. Keep emergency items like orthodontic wax and a distal-end cutter ready for loose or poking wires. Always confirm with your supervising dentist which clinical tasks, including exposing radiographs and adjusting appliances, are within your legal scope.

Summary

Orthodontics corrects malocclusion and irregularities of the teeth and jaws to improve function, hygiene, and appearance. Malocclusion arises from genetic and environmental factors and is classified using Angle's molar relationships (Class I, II, III), with terms like overjet and overbite describing specific patterns. A full set of records, including cephalometric radiographs and study models, guides treatment. Fixed appliances use bands, brackets, archwires, and ligatures, while removable options include aligners and retainers, and retention prevents relapse. The dental assistant supports records and appliances and, above all, teaches the meticulous hygiene that protects teeth during treatment. Always verify state-specific scope for clinical orthodontic tasks.
