Oral and maxillofacial surgery (OMFS) is the dental specialty that diagnoses and treats diseases, injuries, and defects of the hard and soft tissues of the mouth, jaws, and face. As a dental assistant supporting surgical procedures, you help maintain a sterile field, anticipate the surgeon's needs, monitor the patient, and provide clear postoperative instructions. This lesson introduces the surgical setting, common procedures, instruments, and the assistant's responsibilities before, during, and after surgery.

Learning objectives

  • Describe the scope of oral and maxillofacial surgery and the members of the surgical team.
  • Identify common surgical procedures, including simple and surgical extractions.
  • Recognize and name common surgical instruments and their uses.
  • Explain the principles of surgical (sterile) asepsis in the surgical setting.
  • Describe the dental assistant's role during the preoperative, intraoperative, and postoperative phases.
  • List signs and management steps for common postoperative complications such as alveolar osteitis (dry socket).

Scope of oral and maxillofacial surgery

Oral and maxillofacial surgeons complete dental school followed by a hospital-based surgical residency. Their practice ranges from routine tooth removal to complex reconstructive and corrective work. Common categories of treatment include:

  • Extractions — removal of erupted, impacted, or fractured teeth.
  • Impacted third molars — removal of wisdom teeth that cannot fully erupt.
  • Dental implant surgery — placement of titanium fixtures in bone.
  • Biopsy — removal of tissue for pathologic examination.
  • Treatment of facial trauma — repair of fractured jaws and facial bones.
  • Orthognathic (corrective jaw) surgery and treatment of TMJ disorders.

The surgical team

The team typically includes the surgeon, a surgical assistant who maintains the sterile field and passes instruments, and a circulating assistant who handles items outside the sterile field. Surgical asepsis

Surgical procedures break the protective barrier of the oral mucosa, so the standard rises from medical asepsis (clean technique) to surgical asepsis (sterile technique). Key principles include:

  • Sterile instruments and supplies are handled only with sterile gloves or sterile transfer forceps.
  • A sterile field is set up on a sterile drape; once established, only sterile items touch it.
  • Surgical hand antisepsis (a surgical scrub) is performed before gloving.
  • Sterile gowns, masks, and sterile gloves are worn by team members at the sterile field.
  • If sterility is in doubt, the item is considered contaminated and replaced.

Common surgical instruments

Knowing instruments by name and function lets you anticipate the surgeon's next request.

  • Elevators — luxate (loosen) a tooth from the periodontal ligament and surrounding bone before removal; periosteal elevators reflect the soft-tissue flap from bone.
  • Forceps — grasp and remove the tooth; designs are specific to maxillary or mandibular and anterior or posterior teeth.
  • Surgical curette — removes debris, granulation tissue, or a cyst from the socket.
  • Rongeurs and bone files — trim and smooth bone.
  • Scalpel — incises soft tissue; the blade is loaded and removed with a hemostat, never fingers.
  • Hemostat and needle holder — clamp tissue/vessels and place sutures.
  • Surgical suction (Frazier tip) — a narrow tip that keeps the field clear of blood and irrigant.
  • Retractors — hold back the cheek, tongue, or flap for visibility.

Sutures

Sutures approximate tissue and control bleeding after surgery. They may be resorbable (dissolve over time) or nonresorbable (require removal, typically in about 5 to 7 days). The assistant's role through the procedure

Preoperative

  • Review the medical history and confirm any premedication or required consent.
  • Verify the patient has followed pre-surgical instructions (for example, fasting before sedation).
  • Set up the sterile tray and confirm all instruments and supplies are present.

Intraoperative

  • Maintain the sterile field and pass instruments efficiently.
  • Keep the field clear with suction and retraction.
  • Monitor the patient and assist with monitoring vital signs, especially during sedation. [VERIFY: Amanda] (RDA role with sedation monitoring in Texas.)

Postoperative

  • Have the patient bite on gauze to control bleeding.
  • Give written and verbal post-op instructions and confirm the patient understands them.
  • Document the procedure and clean and break down the operatory using PPE.

Postoperative instructions and complications

Typical instructions include biting on gauze, applying cold packs to reduce swelling, eating soft foods, avoiding smoking and drinking through a straw, and not vigorously rinsing on the first day. A key complication is alveolar osteitis (dry socket), a painful condition that arises when the blood clot is lost or fails to form, usually 2 to 4 days after extraction. It is managed by the surgeon, often with a medicated dressing; the assistant reinforces clot-protective instructions to help prevent it.

Key terms

  • Luxation — loosening of a tooth in its socket.
  • Impacted tooth — a tooth that cannot fully erupt into normal position.
  • Surgical asepsis — sterile technique that eliminates all microorganisms.
  • Alveoloplasty — surgical reshaping and smoothing of the bony ridge.
  • Alveolar osteitis (dry socket) — painful loss of the post-extraction blood clot.
  • Resorbable suture — suture material that dissolves and does not require removal.
  • Hemostat — a locking instrument used to clamp tissue or vessels.

Chairside notes

Chairside tip: Set surgical instruments on the tray in the order of use and keep the suction tip out of the surgeon's line of sight while still keeping the field clear. Load and unload scalpel blades with a hemostat, never your fingers, and confirm the gauze count and instrument count before and after the procedure so nothing is left behind. Always confirm with your supervising dentist which intraoperative tasks fall within your legal scope.

Summary

Oral and maxillofacial surgery treats conditions of the mouth, jaws, and face, from simple extractions to facial trauma and corrective jaw surgery. Because surgery breaches the mucosal barrier, the surgical setting demands strict surgical asepsis and a sterile field. The dental assistant supports the surgeon through every phase: preparing the sterile tray, maintaining the field with suction and retraction, monitoring the patient, and delivering clear postoperative instructions. Understanding instruments, sutures, and complications such as dry socket helps you anticipate needs and keep patients safe. Always confirm state-specific scope before performing any intraoperative task.
