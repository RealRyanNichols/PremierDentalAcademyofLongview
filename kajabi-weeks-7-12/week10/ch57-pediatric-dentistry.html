Pediatric dentistry focuses on the oral health of infants, children, and adolescents, including patients with special health care needs. Children are not simply small adults: their teeth, behavior, and emotional needs differ from those of adults, and care is delivered with growth, development, and prevention in mind. This lesson covers the pediatric patient, behavior guidance, common preventive and restorative procedures, and the dental assistant's role in creating a positive, safe experience.

Learning objectives

  • Describe the scope of pediatric dentistry and the importance of the first dental visit.
  • Explain growth and development concepts relevant to the primary and mixed dentition.
  • Identify common behavior guidance techniques used with pediatric patients.
  • Describe common preventive and restorative procedures, including the stainless steel crown and pulp therapy.
  • Recognize the signs of child abuse and neglect and the dental team's reporting responsibility.
  • Explain the dental assistant's role in supporting the pediatric patient and caregiver.

The pediatric patient

The American Academy of Pediatric Dentistry recommends that a child's first dental visit occur by the eruption of the first tooth or by the first birthday, whichever comes first. Early visits establish a "dental home," allow for caregiver education, and catch problems early. Primary (baby) teeth are important: they hold space for permanent teeth, support chewing and speech, and contribute to facial development. Premature loss of a primary tooth can lead to space loss and crowding.

Growth and development

Children move through stages of dentition: the primary dentition (about ages 6 months to 6 years), the mixed dentition (about ages 6 to 12 years, when primary and permanent teeth are both present), and the permanent dentition. Understanding these stages helps the team monitor eruption, spacing, and developing occlusion.

Behavior guidance

Behavior guidance helps a child cope with treatment and build a positive attitude toward dental care. Common nonpharmacologic techniques include:

  • Tell-show-do — explain a procedure in simple words, demonstrate it, then perform it.
  • Voice control — using a calm, measured tone to direct behavior.
  • Positive reinforcement — praising cooperative behavior.
  • Distraction — redirecting the child's attention.
  • Modeling — letting a child observe a cooperative patient.

Pharmacologic options such as nitrous oxide/oxygen analgesia and sedation are used for some children. Preventive and restorative procedures

Prevention

  • Oral hygiene instruction tailored to the child's age and caregiver involvement.
  • Topical fluoride such as fluoride varnish to strengthen enamel.
  • Dental sealants on caries-susceptible pits and fissures (covered in detail in the sealants lesson).
  • Diet counseling and guidance on reducing sugary, frequent snacking. Caregivers are also counseled against putting a child to bed with a bottle of milk or juice to prevent early childhood caries.

Restorative

  • Restorations for caries, using tooth-colored or other appropriate materials.
  • Stainless steel crown (SSC) — a prefabricated crown used to restore a primary tooth with extensive decay or after pulp therapy.
  • Space maintainer — an appliance placed after early loss of a primary tooth to preserve space for the permanent successor.

Pulp therapy

When decay reaches the pulp of a primary tooth, pulp therapy may be needed:

  • Pulpotomy — removal of the coronal (crown) portion of the pulp while preserving healthy radicular (root) pulp; often followed by a stainless steel crown.
  • Pulpectomy — removal of pulp tissue from both the crown and the canals of a primary tooth.

Trauma and special considerations

Dental trauma is common in children. An avulsed (knocked-out) permanent tooth is a true emergency: the tooth should be handled by the crown (not the root), kept moist in an appropriate storage medium such as milk or saline, and the child should be seen immediately. Avulsed primary teeth are generally not reimplanted. The team also adapts care for children with special health care needs.

Recognizing abuse and neglect

Because the head and neck are common sites of inflicted injury, the dental team may be the first to notice signs of child abuse or neglect, such as injuries inconsistent with the explanation given, bruising at various stages of healing, or untreated rampant decay (dental neglect). Dental professionals are mandated reporters. The specific reporting agency, threshold, and procedure are set by state law. Key terms

  • Dental home — an ongoing relationship between the patient and the dental team for comprehensive care.
  • Primary dentition — the set of 20 baby teeth.
  • Mixed dentition — the stage when both primary and permanent teeth are present.
  • Tell-show-do — a behavior guidance technique of explaining, demonstrating, then performing.
  • Pulpotomy — removal of the coronal pulp with preservation of the radicular pulp.
  • Stainless steel crown — a prefabricated crown for restoring primary teeth.
  • Space maintainer — an appliance that holds space after early loss of a primary tooth.
  • Early childhood caries — a pattern of severe decay in young children, linked to frequent sugary liquids.

Chairside notes

Chairside tip: Speak to children at their eye level with simple, nonthreatening words ("sleepy juice" or "tooth pillow" per your office's vocabulary), and use tell-show-do for every new step. Keep instruments out of the child's direct view and praise cooperative behavior specifically. Build rapport with the caregiver too, since a calm parent helps produce a calm child. Always confirm with your supervising dentist which monitoring or sedation-support tasks fall within your legal scope.

Summary

Pediatric dentistry cares for children from infancy through adolescence with attention to growth, prevention, and behavior. Establishing an early dental home, protecting primary teeth, and counseling caregivers on diet and hygiene reduce disease. Behavior guidance techniques such as tell-show-do help children cope, while restorative options like stainless steel crowns, pulp therapy, and space maintainers address decay and tooth loss. The dental assistant supports the child and caregiver, helps maintain safety, and must recognize and report suspected abuse or neglect according to state law. Always verify state-specific scope and reporting requirements.
