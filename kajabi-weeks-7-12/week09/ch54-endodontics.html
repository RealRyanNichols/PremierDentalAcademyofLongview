Endodontics is the dental specialty concerned with the diagnosis and treatment of diseases and injuries of the dental pulp and the periapical (around the root tip) tissues. The most familiar endodontic procedure is root canal therapy, which removes infected or damaged pulp, disinfects the canal system, and seals it—saving a tooth that might otherwise be extracted. As a dental assistant, you help with diagnostic testing, isolation, instrument transfer, irrigation, radiography, and patient reassurance.

The dental pulp

The pulp is the soft tissue in the center of the tooth, housed in the pulp chamber (crown) and root canals (roots). It contains nerves, blood vessels, connective tissue, and odontoblasts. Pulp functions include forming dentin (formative), sensing stimuli such as pain and temperature (sensory), nourishing the dentin (nutritive), and responding to injury (defensive/protective by forming reparative dentin).

Causes of pulpal damage

  • Physical/mechanical: deep caries, trauma (fracture or blow), repeated dental procedures.
  • Thermal: heat from cavity preparation, conduction through large metal restorations.
  • Chemical: irritation from certain materials or acids.
  • Bacterial: microorganisms reaching the pulp through caries, cracks, or leaking restorations—the most common cause.

Pulpal and periapical diagnoses

  • Reversible pulpitis: mild, short-lived inflammation; the pulp can recover if the irritant is removed.
  • Irreversible pulpitis: the pulp is too damaged to heal, often causing lingering or spontaneous pain; requires root canal therapy or extraction.
  • Pulp necrosis: death of the pulp tissue.
  • Periapical abscess: a localized collection of pus at the root apex from bacterial infection.
  • Periapical granuloma / cyst: chronic inflammatory lesions at the apex.

Diagnostic tests

The dentist combines patient history with several tests to reach a diagnosis:

  • Radiographs: reveal caries depth, canal anatomy, and periapical changes. Radiographs are also taken during treatment to confirm working length and obturation.   • Percussion: tapping the tooth to assess tenderness (indicates periapical inflammation).
  • Palpation: pressing on the tissue over the apex.
  • Thermal tests: applying cold or heat to gauge pulp vitality.
  • Electric pulp testing (EPT): a small electrical stimulus to determine whether the pulp responds.
  • Mobility tests: checking tooth movement.

Root canal therapy steps

  1. Anesthesia and isolation: the tooth is anesthetized and isolated, usually with a dental dam, to keep the field clean, dry, and aseptic.
  2. Access opening: the dentist creates an opening through the crown into the pulp chamber.
  3. Pulp removal and canal location: diseased pulp is removed and the canal orifices are located.
  4. Working length determination: measured with files and radiographs or an electronic apex locator to find the canal length to the apex.
  5. Cleaning and shaping: files (hand or rotary) and irrigation remove debris and shape the canals.
  6. Irrigation/disinfection: solutions such as sodium hypochlorite flush and disinfect the canal system.
  7. Obturation (filling): canals are dried and filled, typically with gutta-percha and a sealer, to seal the space.
  8. Restoration: the access is restored; posterior teeth often need a crown to prevent fracture.

Endodontic instruments and materials

  • Files and reamers (K-files, Hedstrom, rotary NiTi): clean and shape the canals.
  • Rubber stops: markers placed on files to indicate working length.
  • Barbed broach: removes pulp tissue.
  • Endodontic explorer and spoon excavator: locate orifices and remove tissue.
  • Gutta-percha: the rubber-like material used to fill canals.
  • Sealer: cement that seals between gutta-percha and canal walls.
  • Irrigants (e.g., sodium hypochlorite): disinfect and flush debris.
  • Apex locator: electronically measures canal length.
  • Spreaders and pluggers: condense the filling material.

Surgical and emergency procedures

  • Apicoectomy: surgical removal of the root apex and surrounding infected tissue when conventional treatment is insufficient.
  • Pulpotomy: removal of the coronal (chamber) pulp while preserving vital root pulp—common in primary teeth.
  • Pulpectomy: complete removal of pulp from the chamber and canals.
  • Incision and drainage: relieving an acute abscess.
  • Retreatment: redoing a previous root canal that has failed.

Key terms

  • Pulp — soft tissue containing nerves and vessels inside the tooth.
  • Pulpitis — inflammation of the pulp (reversible or irreversible).
  • Necrosis — death of pulp tissue.
  • Periapical — region around the root apex.
  • Obturation — filling and sealing the canal system.
  • Gutta-percha — the primary canal filling material.
  • Working length — measured distance from a reference point to the apex.
  • Apex locator — device that electronically measures canal length.
  • Apicoectomy — surgical removal of the root tip.
  • Pulpotomy / pulpectomy — partial / complete pulp removal.

Chairside notes

Chairside notes: Keep the field dry and aseptic—dental dam isolation is the standard of care for root canals. Sodium hypochlorite irrigant is effective but caustic; protect the patient's eyes and clothing, use it carefully, and never let it contact skin or be expressed beyond the apex. Maintain an organized instrument sequence and keep files in order on a measured stand so working length is preserved. Reassure patients—many fear root canals, but with modern anesthesia the procedure is typically comfortable, and good chairside communication eases anxiety.

Summary

Endodontics treats disease of the pulp and periapical tissues, most often through root canal therapy. The pulp can be damaged by bacteria, trauma, thermal, chemical, or mechanical insults, producing diagnoses ranging from reversible pulpitis to necrosis and periapical abscess. Diagnosis relies on history plus radiographic, percussion, palpation, thermal, electric, and mobility tests. Root canal therapy follows a sequence of anesthesia and isolation, access, cleaning and shaping, irrigation, obturation with gutta-percha, and final restoration. The assistant supports isolation, instrument transfer, irrigation safety, radiography, and patient comfort. Always verify Texas RDA radiography and scope requirements before students perform clinical or imaging tasks.
