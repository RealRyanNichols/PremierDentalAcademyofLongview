Periodontics is the dental specialty that focuses on the periodontium—the supporting structures of the teeth—and the prevention, diagnosis, and treatment of periodontal disease. The periodontium includes the gingiva, periodontal ligament, cementum, and alveolar bone. Because periodontal disease is one of the most common reasons adults lose teeth, dental assistants play an important role in patient education, charting, instrument support, and follow-up care.

Learning objectives

  • Identify the structures of the periodontium and their functions.
  • Distinguish between gingivitis and periodontitis.
  • Describe the role of dental biofilm (plaque), calculus, and risk factors in periodontal disease.
  • Explain how a periodontal examination is performed, including probing.
  • Outline nonsurgical and surgical periodontal treatments.
  • Describe the dental assistant's role in periodontal care and patient education.

The periodontium

  • Gingiva: the gum tissue surrounding the teeth; healthy gingiva is firm, pink (or pigmented), and stippled.
  • Periodontal ligament (PDL): connective tissue fibers that attach the tooth (via cementum) to the alveolar bone and cushion occlusal forces.
  • Cementum: the calcified tissue covering the root that anchors the PDL fibers.
  • Alveolar bone: the bone of the jaws that forms the tooth sockets.

Periodontal disease

Periodontal disease is driven primarily by dental biofilm (plaque)—a sticky film of bacteria on tooth surfaces. If not removed, plaque mineralizes into calculus (tartar), which harbors more bacteria and irritates tissues.

Gingivitis

Gingivitis is inflammation of the gingiva without loss of attachment or bone. Signs include red, swollen gingiva that bleeds easily. Gingivitis is generally reversible with improved plaque control and professional cleaning.

Periodontitis

If gingivitis progresses, inflammation extends to the deeper periodontal structures, causing loss of attachment, formation of periodontal pockets, and destruction of alveolar bone. Periodontitis can lead to tooth mobility and eventual tooth loss. Bone and attachment loss are generally not reversible, though the disease can be controlled.

Risk factors

  • Poor oral hygiene and plaque/calculus accumulation.
  • Tobacco use.
  • Systemic conditions such as diabetes.
  • Certain medications, hormonal changes, and genetic susceptibility.

Periodontal examination

A thorough periodontal assessment includes:

  • Probing depths: a calibrated periodontal probe measures the sulcus/pocket depth at multiple sites per tooth; readings are recorded on a perio chart.
  • Bleeding on probing: an indicator of inflammation.
  • Clinical attachment level: assesses true attachment loss.
  • Gingival recession: measured where the gingival margin has receded.
  • Mobility and furcation involvement: evaluating loosening and bone loss between roots.
  • Radiographs: reveal bone levels and calculus.

Periodontal treatment

Nonsurgical therapy

  • Prophylaxis: routine professional cleaning to remove plaque and calculus above the gumline.
  • Scaling and root planing (SRP): deep cleaning that removes plaque and calculus from below the gumline and smooths root surfaces; often called "deep cleaning."
  • Antimicrobials: locally placed or systemic agents may be used as adjuncts.
  • Periodontal maintenance: more frequent recall cleanings to control disease after active treatment.

Surgical therapy

  • Gingivectomy: surgical removal of excess or diseased gingival tissue.
  • Flap surgery (osseous surgery): the gingiva is reflected to access roots and bone for cleaning and reshaping, then sutured back.
  • Soft tissue grafts: tissue is added to cover recession or augment gingiva.
  • Bone grafts / guided tissue regeneration: procedures to regenerate lost bone and attachment.

Key terms

  • Periodontium — the supporting structures of the teeth.
  • Gingiva — the gum tissue.
  • Periodontal ligament (PDL) — fibers attaching tooth to bone.
  • Cementum — calcified layer on the root.
  • Alveolar bone — bone forming the tooth sockets.
  • Gingival sulcus — space between free gingiva and tooth.
  • Biofilm/plaque — bacterial film on teeth.
  • Calculus — hardened (mineralized) plaque, also called tartar.
  • Gingivitis — reversible gum inflammation without bone loss.
  • Periodontitis — disease with attachment and bone loss.
  • Periodontal pocket — deepened sulcus from disease.
  • Scaling and root planing (SRP) — nonsurgical deep cleaning.

Chairside notes

Chairside notes: Patient education is one of the most powerful tools in periodontics—reinforce daily plaque removal through brushing and interdental cleaning, because professional treatment cannot succeed without home care. When charting, record probing depths accurately and call them clearly so the operator does not have to repeat measurements. Watch for bleeding on probing and signs of inflammation, and remember that systemic conditions like diabetes and habits like tobacco use can worsen disease—note these in the patient's history and support tailored counseling.

Summary

Periodontics addresses the health of the periodontium—gingiva, periodontal ligament, cementum, and alveolar bone. Disease begins with plaque biofilm that, untreated, hardens into calculus and triggers inflammation. Gingivitis is reversible inflammation limited to the gingiva, while periodontitis involves irreversible attachment and bone loss that can lead to tooth loss. Diagnosis depends on probing depths, bleeding, attachment levels, mobility, and radiographs recorded on a perio chart. Treatment ranges from nonsurgical prophylaxis and scaling and root planing to surgical procedures such as flap surgery and grafting, followed by ongoing maintenance. The assistant contributes through accurate charting, instrument support, and—above all—patient education. Always confirm Texas RDA scope before performing periodontal clinical tasks.
