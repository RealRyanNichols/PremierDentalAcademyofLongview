Fixed prosthodontics restores or replaces teeth with prostheses that are permanently cemented or bonded in place and cannot be removed by the patient. Common fixed restorations include crowns, fixed bridges (fixed partial dentures), inlays, onlays, and veneers. As a dental assistant, you support the dentist through multiple appointments, manage impression and provisional materials, maintain moisture control, and coach patients on caring for these long-lasting restorations.

Learning objectives

  • Define fixed prosthodontics and distinguish it from removable prosthodontics.
  • Identify the types of indirect restorations: full crowns, partial crowns, inlays, onlays, veneers, and fixed bridges.
  • Describe the components of a fixed bridge (abutment, retainer, pontic, connector).
  • Outline the typical preparation and cementation appointment sequence.
  • Explain the assistant's role in fabricating and placing provisional (temporary) restorations.
  • List common restorative materials and their indications.
  • Educate patients on home care for crowns and bridges.

Indirect restorations

Unlike a direct restoration (such as an amalgam or composite placed in one visit), an indirect restoration is fabricated outside the mouth—at a dental laboratory or by an in-office CAD/CAM (computer-aided design/computer-aided manufacturing) system—and then cemented onto the prepared tooth.

Crowns

  • Full crown: covers the entire clinical crown of the tooth; used for extensively damaged or endodontically treated teeth.
  • Partial crowns (3/4 or 7/8 crown): cover most but not all surfaces, preserving more natural tooth structure.

Inlays and onlays

  • Inlay: a cast or milled restoration that fits within the prepared cavity walls, without covering cusps.
  • Onlay: similar to an inlay but extends over one or more cusps, providing greater coverage and cusp protection.

Veneers

Veneers are thin facings of porcelain or composite bonded to the facial surface of anterior teeth to improve esthetics—correcting discoloration, minor spacing, or shape irregularities.

Fixed bridges (fixed partial dentures)

A fixed bridge replaces one or more missing teeth by anchoring an artificial tooth to adjacent natural teeth or implants. Key components include:

  • Abutment: the natural tooth (or implant) that supports the bridge.
  • Retainer: the crown portion that fits over each abutment.
  • Pontic: the artificial tooth that replaces the missing natural tooth.
  • Connector: the part that joins the pontic to the retainers.

Restorative materials

  • All-metal (gold/high-noble alloys): excellent strength and longevity; less esthetic, so often used on posterior teeth.
  • Porcelain-fused-to-metal (PFM): a metal substructure veneered with porcelain for strength plus esthetics.
  • All-ceramic / all-porcelain: highly esthetic, metal-free; modern materials such as zirconia and lithium disilicate offer improved strength.
  • CAD/CAM ceramics: milled chairside or in the lab for same-day or rapid restorations.

Appointment sequence

Most fixed restorations require at least two appointments (unless an in-office CAD/CAM system allows single-visit delivery).

Preparation appointment

  • Anesthesia and shade selection (choose shade before the teeth dehydrate).
  • Tooth preparation by the dentist; the assistant provides retraction, suction, and moisture control.
  • Gingival retraction (retraction cord or other materials) to expose the margins.
  • Final impression (conventional or digital scan) and bite registration.
  • Fabrication and cementation of a provisional restoration to protect the prepared tooth.

Cementation (delivery) appointment

  • Remove the provisional and clean the preparation.
  • Try-in: verify fit, margins, contacts, occlusion, and shade.
  • Cement or bond the final restoration with the appropriate luting agent.
  • Remove excess cement—especially subgingival excess—and verify occlusion.

Provisional (temporary) restorations

Provisionals protect the prepared tooth, maintain space and occlusion, preserve esthetics, and keep the gingiva healthy while the lab fabricates the final restoration. General fabrication notes: provisionals may be made from preformed (polycarbonate or aluminum) crowns or custom-made from acrylic/bis-acryl materials using a preoperative impression or template. They are seated with a temporary (non-permanent) cement so they can be removed easily at delivery.

Key terms

  • Abutment — the tooth or implant that supports a fixed bridge.
  • Pontic — the artificial replacement tooth in a bridge.
  • Retainer — the crown that covers each abutment.
  • Connector — joins the pontic to the retainer.
  • Inlay / Onlay — indirect restorations within / over the cusps.
  • Veneer — thin facing bonded to improve esthetics.
  • Provisional — temporary restoration worn between appointments.
  • Luting agent — cement used to seat an indirect restoration.
  • Gingival retraction — displacing gingiva to expose margins for impressions.
  • CAD/CAM — computer-aided design/manufacturing of restorations.

Chairside notes

Chairside notes: Select the shade early, while teeth are hydrated—dehydrated teeth appear lighter and lead to mismatched restorations. Protect the provisional and instruct patients to avoid sticky or hard foods on that side and to floss by pulling the floss out the side (not snapping it up) so the temporary stays seated. At cementation, thoroughly remove all excess cement, particularly below the gumline, because retained cement is a common cause of gingival inflammation.

Summary

Fixed prosthodontics permanently restores or replaces teeth using crowns, inlays, onlays, veneers, and bridges. A bridge is built from abutments, retainers, pontics, and connectors. Treatment usually spans a preparation appointment (prep, retraction, impression, provisional) and a delivery appointment (try-in and cementation), unless single-visit CAD/CAM is used. Materials range from durable gold alloys to esthetic all-ceramics. The assistant's responsibilities—moisture control, impression handling, provisional fabrication where permitted, cement cleanup, and patient education—directly affect the fit, esthetics, and longevity of the final restoration.
