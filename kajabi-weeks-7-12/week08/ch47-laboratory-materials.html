The dental laboratory — whether an in-office bench or an outside lab — turns impressions and prescriptions into the casts, models, and appliances that treatment depends on. Dental assistants pour and trim models, fabricate custom trays, make bleaching trays, and prepare cases to send to the lab. Knowing how gypsum products, waxes, and other lab materials behave lets you produce accurate work and communicate clearly with technicians. Gypsum products

All dental gypsum products are forms of calcium sulfate hemihydrate made by heating gypsum (calcium sulfate dihydrate). When water is added, the reaction reverses and the material hardens back into a solid dihydrate. The differences between products come from particle shape and porosity, which control how much water is needed — less water means a stronger, denser set.

  • Model plaster (Type II) — weakest and most porous; used for study models, mounting casts, and as a filler. Requires the most water.
  • Dental stone (Type III) — stronger than plaster; used for working casts and diagnostic models, especially for removable prosthodontics.
  • Die stone / high-strength stone (Type IV) — very strong and abrasion-resistant; used for dies and working casts for crowns and bridges. Requires the least water.
  • Type V high-strength, high-expansion stone — even stronger with slightly greater setting expansion, used for certain alloy castings.

The water-to-powder (W/P) ratio is critical: too much water weakens the cast and slows the set; too little produces a thick mix that traps air and is hard to pour. Always weigh or measure powder and water per the manufacturer's directions rather than estimating. Add powder to pre-measured water, mix to a smooth, creamy consistency (often vacuum-mixed to reduce bubbles), and observe the initial set (loss of gloss) and final set before separating.

Pouring and trimming a model

Gently vibrate the mix into the impression in small increments from one corner, letting it flow ahead of itself to avoid trapping air. After the anatomic portion is filled, add a base. Allow the gypsum to reach final set (typically about 45–60 minutes; do not rush) before separating to prevent fracturing teeth. A trimmed diagnostic cast has a smooth, symmetric base and trimmed borders so the casts articulate properly and present professionally. The art portion (anatomy) and the base portion follow standard proportions for a neatly trimmed model.

Setting time and expansion

Warm water and faster spatulation speed the set; cooler water and additives (retarders such as borax) slow it. All gypsum products expand slightly as they set; products are formulated to control this expansion so casts stay dimensionally accurate.

Dental waxes

Waxes are grouped by use:

  • Pattern waxes — inlay/casting wax, baseplate wax, and casting wax used to form patterns for restorations and denture bases.
  • Processing waxes — boxing wax (to form a base around an impression before pouring), utility wax (to extend or build up tray borders for comfort), and sticky wax (to temporarily join pieces).
  • Impression waxes — bite registration wax used to record occlusion, and corrective waxes.

Waxes have a high coefficient of thermal expansion, so they warp easily; handle and store them carefully and pour wax records promptly.

Custom trays and other lab tasks

A custom impression tray is built on a diagnostic cast to fit a specific patient, giving even material thickness for accurate final impressions. Common materials are light-cured resin, self-curing acrylic resin, and thermoplastic (vacuum-formed) sheets. The basic steps: outline the tray border on the cast, place a spacer to allow room for impression material, adapt and cure the tray material, trim and smooth the edges, and add a handle. Always allow acrylic monomer to off-gas and work with proper ventilation. Vacuum-formed machines also produce bleaching trays, mouthguards, and provisional matrices.

Key terms

  • Gypsum — calcium sulfate dihydrate; the base mineral of dental stone and plaster.
  • Calcium sulfate hemihydrate — the powdered form that hardens when water is added.
  • Water-to-powder (W/P) ratio — the proportion of water to gypsum powder; controls strength and set.
  • Die — an accurate replica of a single prepared tooth used to fabricate a restoration.
  • Initial set / final set — stages of gypsum hardening (loss of gloss, then full set).
  • Spacer — material placed on the cast to create room for impression material in a custom tray.
  • Boxing wax — wax used to form a base wall around an impression before pouring.

Chairside notes

Practical tips for the lab bench:

  • Always add powder to water (not water to powder) for a smoother, bubble-free mix.
  • Weigh or measure both components; a sloppy W/P ratio is the top cause of weak, inaccurate casts.
  • Vibrate the mix slowly into the impression from one corner to chase air out ahead of the flow.
  • Be patient — separating before final set chips teeth off the cast and wastes the appointment.
  • Disinfect impressions before pouring and disinfect cases before sending to or receiving from the lab.
  • Store gypsum powder sealed and dry; moisture absorbed from the air alters its set.
  • Label every case with the patient name, prescription, and shade information, and double-check the lab slip. [VERIFY: Amanda — office lab-prescription and disinfection protocol.]

Summary

Laboratory materials — chiefly gypsum products and waxes — convert clinical records into the casts and appliances that drive treatment. Gypsum products differ mainly in strength and water demand: plaster for study models, stone for working casts, and die stone for crown-and-bridge dies. Accurate work depends on a correct water-to-powder ratio, proper mixing and vibration, and patience during setting. Waxes serve patterning, processing, and registration roles but warp with heat. Careful case handling, infection control, and clear lab communication keep laboratory work accurate and predictable.
