When a tooth is prepared for an indirect restoration such as a crown, inlay, onlay, or bridge, the definitive restoration is fabricated in the laboratory and cemented at a later visit. In the meantime, the prepared tooth must be protected by a provisional (temporary) restoration. Provisionals are a routine, high-value chairside skill: a well-made temporary keeps the patient comfortable, protects the preparation, and holds everything in place so the final restoration fits. Learning objectives

  • State the functions and requirements of a provisional restoration.
  • Differentiate custom (direct/indirect) and preformed provisional techniques.
  • Identify the materials used to fabricate provisionals.
  • Describe how to fabricate, trim, fit, and temporarily cement a provisional.
  • List the criteria for an acceptable provisional and recognize problems.
  • Give appropriate post-placement instructions to the patient.

Purpose and requirements of provisionals

A provisional restoration serves several jobs at once:

  • Protection — covers exposed dentin, reducing sensitivity and shielding the pulp.
  • Position — prevents the prepared tooth and its neighbors/opposing teeth from drifting or supra-erupting, which would prevent the final restoration from seating.
  • Function and comfort — restores the ability to chew and maintains the occlusion.
  • Esthetics — maintains appearance, especially in the anterior.
  • Gingival health — proper contours and margins keep the gingiva healthy so the final impression and seating are clean.

To do these jobs, a provisional must have accurate margins, correct contours and contacts, harmonious occlusion, a smooth polished surface, and it must be retained yet easily removable for the seating appointment.

Techniques and materials

Custom provisionals

Custom provisionals are shaped to the patient's own anatomy, usually from a matrix taken before the tooth is prepared. Common approaches:

  • Preoperative impression / matrix technique (direct) — an alginate or putty impression (or a vacuum-formed shell) is taken of the tooth before preparation. After the tooth is prepared, provisional resin is placed in the matrix, seated over the preparation, allowed to set briefly, removed, trimmed, and finished. This reproduces the original contours closely.
  • Indirect technique — the provisional is made on a model away from the mouth (less common chairside).

Common custom materials are acrylic and composite/bis-acryl provisional resins. Bis-acryl (composite-based) resins are popular because they are easy to use in an automix gun, produce less heat and odor, and finish well. Methyl methacrylate and other acrylic resins are durable and economical but generate more heat during setting (protect the tooth) and have a stronger odor.

Preformed provisionals

Preformed (prefabricated) crowns are stocked in standard shapes and sizes and adapted to the tooth:

  • Polycarbonate crowns — tooth-colored shells for anterior teeth.
  • Aluminum and tin-silver shells — metal temporaries for posterior teeth.
  • Stainless steel crowns — durable; often used on primary teeth and as posterior temporaries.
  • Clear celluloid crown forms — used as a matrix to be filled with resin.

Preformed crowns are selected by size, trimmed and contoured to fit the margin and occlusion, then cemented with temporary cement.

Fabrication, fit, and temporary cementation

Whatever the technique, the general sequence is: capture or select the form, fill or adapt with provisional material, seat and let set, remove and trim excess, adjust the margins, contacts, and occlusion, then polish. Check the occlusion with articulating paper — a high provisional causes pain and can fracture. Verify contacts so food does not pack and adjacent teeth do not shift. Cement with temporary (provisional) cement, commonly zinc oxide eugenol–based or a non-eugenol temporary cement, then remove all excess cement, especially from the sulcus, to protect the gingiva.

Key terms

  • Provisional / temporary restoration — an interim restoration worn until the final is placed.
  • Matrix — a mold or shell used to shape the provisional (putty, alginate, vacuum-formed, or celluloid).
  • Bis-acryl resin — a composite-based provisional material delivered by automix gun.
  • Polycarbonate crown — a tooth-colored preformed temporary crown for anterior teeth.
  • Temporary cement — a weak cement (often ZOE-based) that retains a provisional yet allows easy removal.
  • Supra-eruption — over-eruption of an unopposed or unrestored tooth.
  • Contour — the shape of the restoration that supports gingival health and proper contacts.

Chairside notes

Practical tips for fabricating provisionals:

  • Take the preoperative matrix BEFORE the tooth is prepared — you cannot recreate the original anatomy after the fact.
  • With acrylic, the exothermic set can heat the tooth; remove and reseat the provisional during setting (or use cool water) to dissipate heat and prevent lock-on over undercuts.
  • Always adjust occlusion with articulating paper; a high temporary is the most common patient complaint.
  • Polish thoroughly — a smooth surface resists plaque and irritates the gingiva less.
  • Remove every trace of excess temporary cement from the sulcus to prevent gingival inflammation.
  • Use non-eugenol temporary cement when the final restoration will be bonded with resin cement, because residual eugenol can interfere with bonding. [VERIFY: Amanda — office cement selection protocol.]
  • Tell the patient to expect possible sensitivity, to avoid sticky/hard foods, to chew on the other side, and to call if the provisional comes off.

Summary

Provisional restorations protect the prepared tooth, hold its position and occlusion, maintain esthetics and gingival health, and keep the patient comfortable between the preparation and seating appointments. They are made by custom techniques (a preoperative matrix filled with acrylic or bis-acryl resin) or with preformed crowns sized and adapted to the tooth, then cemented with temporary cement. An acceptable provisional has accurate margins, correct contacts and contours, balanced occlusion, and a polished surface, with all excess cement removed. Strong provisional skills protect both the tooth and the success of the final restoration.
