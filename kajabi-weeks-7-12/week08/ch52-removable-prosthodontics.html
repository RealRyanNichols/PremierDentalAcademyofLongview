Removable prosthodontics replaces missing teeth and surrounding structures with appliances the patient can take out and put back in. The two major categories are the removable partial denture (RPD), for a patient who still has some natural teeth, and the complete (full) denture, for an arch with no remaining teeth. Dental assistants support every stage of these multi-appointment cases — impressions, records, try-ins, delivery, and education — so understanding the components, sequence, and patient care is essential. Removable partial dentures (RPD)

An RPD restores one or more missing teeth in an arch that still has natural teeth and is indicated when the spaces are too large for a fixed bridge, when there are no posterior abutments, for financial reasons, or when adjacent teeth cannot support a bridge. RPDs are removable for cleaning and rely on the remaining teeth and tissue for support and stability.

Components of an RPD

  • Framework — the cast metal (or resin) skeleton that connects all the parts.
  • Major connector — the rigid bar or plate that joins the components on each side of the arch (for example, a lingual bar or palatal strap).
  • Minor connectors — smaller arms that attach other components to the major connector.
  • Rests — extensions that sit on prepared seats in the abutment teeth to direct chewing forces down the long axis and prevent the RPD from sinking into tissue.
  • Retainers (clasps) — arms that grip the abutment teeth to hold the RPD in place.
  • Abutment teeth — the natural teeth that support and retain the prosthesis.
  • Denture base and artificial (pontic/replacement) teeth — the resin base carrying the replacement teeth over the edentulous areas.

Complete dentures

A complete denture replaces all the teeth in an arch and rests on the residual ridge and supporting tissues. The maxillary denture gains retention partly from a seal across the palate; the mandibular denture is harder to retain because of the tongue and a smaller bearing area. An immediate denture is placed the same day the remaining teeth are extracted so the patient is not without teeth, but it later requires relining as the ridge heals and resorbs. An overdenture fits over retained roots or implants for added support and stability.

Denture materials

Most denture bases are acrylic resin (polymethyl methacrylate), processed pink to mimic gingiva. Artificial teeth are made of acrylic resin or porcelain; acrylic teeth bond to the resin base, wear more like natural teeth, and are quieter, while porcelain teeth are very wear-resistant but more brittle and can be noisy. RPD frameworks are usually a cast cobalt-chromium or other dental alloy for strength and rigidity.

Appointment sequence and the assistant's role

Removable prostheses are made over several visits. A typical sequence:

  • Examination and diagnosis — preliminary (diagnostic) impressions, radiographs, and treatment planning. The assistant takes preliminary impressions and pours diagnostic casts.
  • Final impressions — often using custom trays; for partials, abutment teeth may be prepared with rest seats. The assistant prepares trays and materials and pours casts.
  • Jaw relation / bite registration records — baseplates and wax occlusion rims establish the vertical dimension, midline, and how the arches relate. The assistant prepares the rims and materials.
  • Try-in (wax setup) — the teeth are set in wax for the dentist and patient to verify fit, occlusion, shade, and esthetics before processing. The assistant prepares the setup and mirror for evaluation.
  • Delivery (insertion) — the finished prosthesis is fitted, adjusted for sore spots and occlusion, and the patient is educated.
  • Post-delivery adjustments, relines, and recalls — sore spots are relieved; over time, relines or rebases compensate for ridge resorption.

Key terms

  • Abutment — a natural tooth that supports and retains a partial denture.
  • Clasp (retainer) — the arm that grips an abutment to hold an RPD in place.
  • Rest — a projection seated on an abutment to direct forces and prevent tissue impingement.
  • Major connector — the rigid component joining the two sides of an RPD.
  • Edentulous — without teeth.
  • Immediate denture — a denture placed the same day the teeth are removed.
  • Overdenture — a denture supported by retained roots or implants.
  • Reline / rebase — refitting the tissue surface of a denture to the changed ridge.
  • Residual ridge — the bone and tissue remaining after teeth are removed.

Chairside notes

Practical tips for removable cases:

  • Use dental stone (Type III) for sturdy, accurate working casts on removable cases.
  • Keep accurate, well-labeled records and casts; removable cases span many visits and a lost record costs an appointment.
  • Handle dentures over a folded towel or a basin of water — acrylic fractures easily if dropped on a hard counter.
  • At delivery, teach the patient to insert and remove the prosthesis with fingers (not by biting it into place, which bends clasps).
  • Instruct daily cleaning over water or a towel with a denture brush and cleaner, not abrasive toothpaste, and to remove the denture at night unless the dentist directs otherwise to let the tissues rest.
  • Set expectations: new dentures need an adjustment period; speech, chewing, and possible sore spots improve, and the patient should return for adjustments rather than self-adjusting the appliance.
  • Remind partial-denture patients to keep their remaining natural teeth and abutments meticulously clean to prevent decay and gum disease. [VERIFY: Amanda — office home-care and recall protocol.]

Summary

Removable prosthodontics restores function and esthetics for patients missing some or all of their teeth. Partial dentures replace several teeth in a dentate arch using a framework with major and minor connectors, rests, and clasps anchored to abutment teeth, while complete dentures replace an entire arch and rest on the residual ridge. Most are built from acrylic resin with acrylic or porcelain teeth, fabricated over several appointments — impressions, records, try-in, delivery, and follow-up. The assistant supports each step and is central to patient education on insertion, removal, cleaning, and realistic expectations, all of which determine how well the patient adapts to the prosthesis.
