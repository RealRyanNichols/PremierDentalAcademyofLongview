Dental impressions capture a negative reproduction of the teeth and surrounding oral structures so the laboratory can produce an accurate positive model (cast). As a dental assistant, you will mix, load, and frequently take preliminary impressions, and you will support the dentist during final impressions for crowns, bridges, dentures, and other prostheses. Understanding how each material behaves — and how a tiny error can ruin a case — is essential chairside knowledge. Purpose and classification of impressions

A preliminary impression is used for diagnostic casts, study models, custom tray fabrication, provisional restorations, opposing models, and bleaching trays. A final (master) impression reproduces fine detail for the working cast used to fabricate the definitive restoration. A bite registration records how the upper and lower arches occlude.

Impression materials are classified two ways. By how they set: reversible (set by a physical change that can be undone, such as agar hydrocolloid) versus irreversible (set by a chemical reaction that cannot be reversed, such as alginate and elastomers). By their set physical property: elastic materials (hydrocolloids and elastomers) can be drawn over undercuts and rebound, while inelastic materials (impression plaster, impression compound, zinc oxide eugenol) cannot.

Hydrocolloid materials

Irreversible hydrocolloid (alginate) is the most commonly used preliminary impression material. It is supplied as a powder mixed with water; the powder contains potassium alginate, calcium sulfate (a reactor), and other modifiers. Mixing triggers a gelation reaction. Alginate is inexpensive, comfortable, and hydrophilic, but it tears easily, distorts if not poured promptly, and lacks the fine detail needed for final impressions. Reversible hydrocolloid (agar) gels and liquefies with temperature changes, requires specialized conditioning equipment, and is used less often today.

Elastomeric (final) impression materials

Elastomers are rubber-like materials used for final impressions. The major types are:

  • Polyvinyl siloxane (PVS / addition silicone) — the most popular final material. Excellent accuracy, dimensional stability, and tear resistance; available in light, medium, heavy, and putty viscosities for one- or two-step techniques. It can be poured multiple times. Latex gloves and some retraction-cord chemicals can inhibit its set, so handling matters.
  • Polyether — very accurate and hydrophilic, ideal in moist fields, but stiff when set (difficult removal over deep undercuts) and absorbs water if stored wet.
  • Condensation silicone — older silicone that releases a byproduct and shrinks over time; pour promptly.
  • Polysulfide — accurate with a long working time, but messy, odorous, and slow setting.

Most elastomers come as a base and catalyst that are mixed by hand, by automix gun (cartridge), or by machine. Viscosity selection follows the technique: a low-viscosity (light-body) wash captures fine margins while a high-viscosity (heavy-body or putty) tray material provides bulk and hydraulic pressure.

Inelastic materials

Zinc oxide eugenol (ZOE) paste records edentulous ridges with no undercuts. Impression compound is thermoplastic and used to border-mold trays. Impression plaster is rarely used now. These cannot be withdrawn over undercuts without fracturing.

Controlling set and accuracy

Warmer water and warmer environments speed the set; cooler water slows it — useful for controlling working time. Follow the manufacturer's powder-to-water or base-to-catalyst ratio exactly; guessing ruins accuracy. Alginate impressions must be poured within about 10–15 minutes (or stored in 100% humidity) because they distort through syneresis (weeping/water loss) and imbibition (water absorption and swelling). Elastomers are far more stable but still benefit from a prompt, properly timed pour.

Trays and impression technique

Trays may be stock (perforated or non-perforated, metal or plastic, in arch sizes) or custom (made on a diagnostic cast for a precise fit). Perforations and tray adhesive lock the material to the tray so it does not pull away on removal. Select a tray that covers all needed structures with even clearance, apply adhesive when indicated, load without trapping air, seat over the arch, and hold motionless until fully set. Remove with a quick straight pull to avoid tearing, then rinse, disinfect per protocol, and evaluate.

Key terms

  • Hydrocolloid — a water-based gel material (alginate, agar).
  • Elastomer — a rubber-like final impression material (PVS, polyether, etc.).
  • Imbibition — absorption of water causing a hydrocolloid to swell.
  • Syneresis — loss of water (weeping) causing a hydrocolloid to shrink.
  • Gelation — the change from sol (liquid) to gel (set) state.
  • Viscosity — the thickness/flow of a material (light, medium, heavy, putty).
  • Border molding — shaping impression material to record the functional vestibule.
  • Tray adhesive — coating that bonds impression material to the tray.

Chairside notes

Practical tips for the operatory:

  • Measure water and powder with the supplied scoops/measures — eyeballing ratios is the number-one cause of failed alginate.
  • For PVS, avoid latex gloves and some sulfur-containing materials when handling the unset material; use vinyl or nitrile and confirm the set on a leftover scrap.
  • Load alginate into the tray in bulk and smooth the surface; wiping a thin layer onto the occlusal surfaces first reduces trapped bubbles.
  • Tell the patient to breathe through the nose and lean slightly forward to reduce gag reflex; never leave a patient unattended with a tray seated.
  • Pour or store impressions promptly; label trays and bite registrations clearly for the lab.
  • Disinfect every impression following your office's protocol and the manufacturer's instructions before it leaves the operatory. [VERIFY: Amanda — office-specific disinfection product and contact time.]

Summary

Impressions translate the mouth into a model the lab can work from, and material choice depends on whether you need a quick diagnostic record or a precise final impression. Alginate dominates preliminary work; PVS and polyether dominate final impressions. Success comes from correct material/tray selection, exact mixing ratios, controlled set time, careful seating and removal, and prompt pouring or proper storage. As an assistant, mastering these fundamentals keeps cases on schedule and protects patients from repeat appointments.
