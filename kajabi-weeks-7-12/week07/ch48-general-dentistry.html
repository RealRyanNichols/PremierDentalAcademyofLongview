General dentistry is the foundation of everyday practice and the setting where most dental assistants spend their careers. It covers the routine restorative and operative procedures that restore form, function, and esthetics to teeth damaged by decay, fracture, or wear. This lesson surveys the core procedures of a general practice — amalgam and composite restorations, cavity preparation, and the assistant's role in each — so you can anticipate the dentist's needs and keep procedures moving efficiently.

Learning objectives

  • Describe the scope of general (operative/restorative) dentistry.
  • Compare amalgam and composite resin as direct restorative materials.
  • Outline the steps of a typical cavity preparation and restoration.
  • Explain the principles of moisture control and four-handed dentistry during restorative procedures.
  • Identify the assistant's responsibilities before, during, and after a restorative appointment.

The scope of general dentistry

A general dentist diagnoses and treats a broad range of conditions and refers complex cases to specialists. Operative (restorative) dentistry — the focus of this chapter — deals with restoring individual teeth. Procedures include removing decay, restoring teeth with direct materials placed in a single visit (amalgam or composite), and preparing teeth for indirect restorations such as crowns and inlays. The assistant supports each of these through preparation, instrument transfer, moisture control, and patient care.

Direct restorative materials

Amalgam

Dental amalgam is an alloy of mercury with silver, tin, copper, and other metals. It is strong, wear-resistant, and relatively forgiving of moisture, which has made it a long-standing choice for posterior (load-bearing) restorations. It is silver-colored rather than tooth-colored. Amalgam is mixed (triturated) in a capsule, carried to the preparation with an amalgam carrier, condensed firmly, carved to anatomy, and burnished. Scrap amalgam and capsules must be handled as regulated waste. [VERIFY: Amanda] — confirm current local/federal amalgam waste and mercury-handling requirements.

Composite resin

Composite resin is a tooth-colored material made of a resin matrix filled with glass or quartz particles. It bonds to tooth structure (using the adhesive systems covered in Chapter 44), is highly esthetic, and is now used widely in both anterior and posterior teeth. Composite is placed in increments, each light-cured before the next, then shaped and polished. It is more technique-sensitive than amalgam and demands excellent moisture control because contamination weakens the bond.

Steps of a typical restoration

  1. Anesthesia: The dentist administers local anesthetic; the assistant prepares and transfers the syringe (per state scope) and monitors the patient.
  2. Isolation: A dental dam, cotton rolls, or isolation device is placed to keep the field dry.
  3. Cavity preparation: The dentist removes decay and shapes the preparation with the handpiece while the assistant provides oral evacuation, retraction, and a clear field.
  4. Pulp protection: Liners and bases are placed as needed (see Chapter 44).
  5. Restoration: The material is placed — amalgam is condensed and carved; composite is bonded, incrementally placed, and cured.
  6. Finishing and occlusion: The restoration is contoured and polished, and the bite is checked with articulating paper and adjusted as needed.

Moisture control and four-handed dentistry

Keeping the operating field clean and dry is one of the assistant's most important contributions. The high-volume evacuator (HVE) removes water, saliva, and debris, while the air-water syringe rinses and dries. The dental dam isolates the tooth, improves visibility, protects the airway, and keeps the field free of contamination. Four-handed dentistry — the dentist and seated assistant working together with efficient instrument transfer — reduces appointment time, reduces stress on both clinicians, and improves the quality of care.

Key terms

  • Operative dentistry — the branch of general dentistry concerned with restoring individual teeth.
  • Direct restoration — a restoration placed and shaped directly in the tooth in one visit (amalgam, composite).
  • Indirect restoration — a restoration fabricated outside the mouth, then cemented (crown, inlay, onlay).
  • Amalgam — a strong, silver-colored alloy of mercury and other metals used for restorations.
  • Composite resin — a tooth-colored, bondable filled-resin restorative material.
  • Trituration — mechanical mixing of amalgam in a capsule.
  • Condensation — packing restorative material firmly into the preparation.
  • High-volume evacuator (HVE) — suction device that keeps the field dry and clear.
  • Four-handed dentistry — coordinated work of a seated operator and assistant for efficiency.

Chairside notes

  • Set up the tray in the order the procedure will follow so transfers are predictable.
  • Position the HVE tip near the tooth being worked on — close to the field but out of the dentist's line of sight and away from soft tissue.
  • Triturate amalgam only when the dentist is ready to place it; amalgam has a limited working time.
  • For composite, protect the material from the overhead light, which can begin to cure it prematurely.
  • Keep articulating paper, floss, and finishing burs ready for the occlusal check and polish.
  • Handle and dispose of amalgam scrap as regulated waste; never rinse it down the drain.
  • Watch the patient continuously for signs of discomfort or distress and communicate with the dentist.

Summary

General (operative) dentistry restores damaged teeth using direct materials such as amalgam and composite resin, as well as indirect restorations prepared for later cementation. Amalgam is strong and moisture-tolerant; composite is esthetic and bonded but technique-sensitive. Across every restorative procedure, the assistant's command of moisture control, instrument transfer, and four-handed technique determines how smoothly the visit goes and how comfortable the patient feels.
