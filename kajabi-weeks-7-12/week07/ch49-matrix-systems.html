When a cavity involves one or more of the contacting surfaces between teeth (the proximal surfaces), the natural wall of the tooth is missing. A matrix system temporarily replaces that missing wall so the restorative material can be packed against a solid surface, rebuilding a proper contour and a tight contact with the neighboring tooth. Without a matrix, the dentist could not create a normal interproximal contact or a smooth, overhang-free margin. As an assistant, you will assemble, place, and remove matrices, so understanding each component and how it fits together is a core operative skill.

Learning objectives

  • Explain why a matrix is needed for restorations involving proximal surfaces.
  • Identify the components of the Tofflemire (universal) matrix system and how they assemble.
  • Describe sectional matrix systems and when they are used.
  • Explain the purpose of wedges in matrix placement.
  • Describe the matrices used for anterior restorations.
  • Outline the assistant's role in assembling, placing, and removing a matrix.

Why a matrix is needed

A matrix provides the temporary missing wall for a Class II (posterior proximal), Class III, or Class IV (anterior proximal) restoration. It allows the operator to: confine the restorative material to the preparation, restore a normal proximal contour, establish a tight contact with the adjacent tooth, and produce a smooth margin without an overhang. A poorly adapted matrix leads to open contacts (which trap food), overhangs (which harbor plaque and irritate the gingiva), and a restoration that must be redone.

The Tofflemire (universal) matrix system

The Tofflemire system is the classic universal matrix for posterior (Class II) amalgam and many composite restorations. Its parts work together to hold a thin metal band tightly around the tooth.

Components

  • Retainer (Tofflemire retainer): the mechanical body that holds and tightens the band. It has an outer (spindle) knob that locks the band into the diagonal slot of the vise and an inner knob that adjusts the size of the loop.
  • Matrix band: a thin, contoured strip of stainless steel. It is wider on one edge to follow the occlusal-gingival contour; the smaller circumference of the loop is the gingival edge.
  • Wedge: a small wooden or plastic wedge placed in the gingival embrasure to adapt the band against the tooth and prevent gingival overhang.

Assembly principles

The band is formed into a teardrop-shaped loop. The guide channels of the retainer are positioned toward the gingiva, and the retainer is positioned so it sits on the buccal (facial) surface, allowing easy removal. The loop's smaller circumference is the gingival edge so the band hugs the tooth at the gum line. Once placed over the tooth, the inner knob is tightened to snug the band.

Sectional matrix systems

Sectional matrices use a small, precontoured metal band for a single proximal surface, held in place by a separating ring (a spring-like ring that wraps around the tooth) plus a wedge. Sectional systems are popular for posterior composite restorations because the ring separates the teeth slightly and the precontoured band helps create a tight, anatomic proximal contact that is often difficult to achieve with a full band.

Wedges

The wedge is inserted from the larger embrasure (usually the lingual) into the gingival embrasure. Its jobs are to: adapt the band tightly at the gingival margin to prevent overhangs, hold the band in place, and provide slight separation of the teeth to compensate for band thickness so a tight contact results after removal. Wedges come in wood and plastic and in several sizes; the size is chosen to fit the embrasure snugly without blanching the tissue excessively.

Anterior matrices

For anterior (Class III and IV) composite restorations, a clear plastic (Mylar) matrix strip is used. Because it is transparent, the curing light can pass through it to cure the composite. It is held against the proximal surface and pulled tight while the composite is shaped and cured, then removed. Anterior wedges may be used to adapt the strip at the gingival margin.

Key terms

  • Matrix — a device that temporarily replaces a missing proximal wall during restoration.
  • Tofflemire retainer — the universal mechanical holder that tightens and positions a matrix band.
  • Matrix band — the thin metal strip that forms the temporary wall.
  • Wedge — a small wood or plastic insert that adapts the band at the gingival margin and slightly separates the teeth.
  • Sectional matrix — a small precontoured band with a separating ring for a single proximal surface.
  • Separating ring — a spring ring that holds a sectional band and separates the teeth.
  • Mylar (clear) strip — a transparent matrix used for anterior composite restorations.
  • Overhang — excess restorative material extending beyond the margin, often at the gingival wall.
  • Proximal contact — the area where adjacent teeth touch.

Chairside notes

  • Place the Tofflemire retainer on the buccal surface so it does not interfere with the tongue and is easy to remove.
  • Position the smaller circumference of the band loop toward the gingiva so it hugs the tooth at the gum line.
  • Burnish the band against the adjacent tooth to help form a positive proximal contact before the material is placed.
  • Seat the wedge firmly from the larger embrasure so the gingival margin is sealed — this is the best defense against overhangs.
  • For posterior composites, a sectional matrix with a separating ring usually gives a tighter, more anatomic contact than a full band.
  • Use a clear Mylar strip for anterior composites so the curing light passes through.
  • Have a clear sequence for removal: loosen the retainer and slide it off, then gently remove the band, then the wedge, so the new restoration is not fractured.

Summary

A matrix system temporarily restores the missing proximal wall so the dentist can rebuild proper contour, contact, and a smooth margin. The Tofflemire (universal) system — retainer, band, and wedge — is the classic choice for posterior restorations, while sectional matrices with separating rings excel at posterior composite contacts and clear Mylar strips serve anterior composites. Correct band orientation, firm wedging, and careful removal are the assistant's keys to a restoration with a tight contact and no overhang.
