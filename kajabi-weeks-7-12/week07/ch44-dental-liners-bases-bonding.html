Before a tooth is restored, the dentist often places protective materials between the prepared tooth structure and the final restoration. These materials — liners, bases, and bonding systems — protect the pulp, reduce sensitivity, and help the restoration stay in place for years. As a dental assistant, you will mix, transfer, and sometimes (where state scope permits) place these materials, so understanding what each one does and why it is chosen is essential to confident chairside work. Why pulpal protection matters

The pulp is living tissue. Deep decay, the heat of cutting, chemical irritation from materials, and temperature changes transmitted through metallic restorations can all injure it. Protective materials create a barrier that insulates the pulp thermally, neutralizes acids, seals the dentinal tubules, and in some cases stimulates the formation of reparative (secondary) dentin. The amount of healthy dentin remaining over the pulp — the remaining dentin thickness — largely determines how much protection a tooth needs: a shallow preparation may need little or none, while a deep one may need a liner and a base.

Cavity liners

A liner is a thin layer of material applied to the deepest portion of the preparation, directly over or very near the pulp. Liners are flowable and set into a thin film; they are not meant to provide structural bulk.

Calcium hydroxide

Calcium hydroxide is a classic liner used in the deepest part of a deep preparation. It is highly alkaline, which makes it antibacterial, and it stimulates the formation of reparative dentin. It is typically supplied as a two-paste (base and catalyst) system that is mixed in equal amounts to a uniform color.

Glass ionomer

Glass ionomer liners chemically bond to tooth structure and release fluoride over time, which helps protect against recurrent decay. Resin-modified glass ionomer (RMGI) versions are light-cured and widely used as liners and bases.

Cavity varnish

Varnish is a thin resin in a volatile solvent that is painted onto the dentin to seal the tubules and reduce microleakage, classically under amalgam restorations. Because varnish can interfere with the bonding of composite and resin-based cements, it is not used with those materials.

Cement bases

A base is a thicker layer placed beneath a permanent restoration to provide thermal insulation, mechanical support, and protection from chemical irritation. Because metallic restorations conduct temperature readily, bases are especially important under amalgam in deeper preparations. Common base materials include reinforced zinc oxide–eugenol, zinc phosphate, and glass ionomer/RMGI. The material is mixed to a putty-like consistency and condensed into place to rebuild the floor of the preparation toward the ideal contour.

Dental bonding (adhesive) systems

Bonding systems create a micromechanical and chemical link between tooth structure and the restorative material, especially composite resin. There are two main approaches:

  • Total-etch (etch-and-rinse): Phosphoric acid (typically about 35–37%) is applied to enamel and dentin, then rinsed away. Etching enamel creates microscopic porosities; etching dentin exposes collagen fibers. A primer and adhesive are then applied and light-cured, forming a resin interlock.
  • Self-etch: An acidic primer etches and primes simultaneously, so there is no separate rinse step. This reduces technique sensitivity and post-operative sensitivity for many cases.

Correct moisture control is critical: enamel is generally bonded dry, while dentin in total-etch systems should be left slightly moist ("wet bonding") so the collagen does not collapse. Each step is light-cured for the manufacturer-specified time.

Key terms

  • Liner — a thin protective layer placed over the deepest dentin near the pulp.
  • Base — a thicker layer that provides thermal insulation and mechanical support under a restoration.
  • Varnish — a thin resin coating that seals dentinal tubules, classically used under amalgam.
  • Remaining dentin thickness — the amount of healthy dentin between the preparation floor and the pulp; guides how much protection is needed.
  • Microleakage — the seepage of fluids and bacteria between the restoration and tooth at the margins.
  • Etching — acid conditioning of enamel/dentin to create a surface for micromechanical bonding.
  • Primer — a material that wets and penetrates etched dentin to prepare it for the adhesive resin.
  • Reparative dentin — new dentin formed by the pulp in response to a stimulus.

Chairside notes

  • Read the manufacturer's instructions for every product — mix ratios, working time, and cure times vary by brand.
  • Keep the field dry and isolated; saliva contamination ruins bonding and weakens cement.
  • Mix two-paste calcium hydroxide to a single uniform color and apply only a thin film to the deepest spot — not the entire preparation.
  • Do not use cavity varnish under composite or resin cements; it blocks adhesion.
  • Have the curing light, light-protective shield, and applicators ready before bonding begins so steps are not delayed.
  • Cap etchant and bonding agent bottles immediately; bonding resins are light- and air-sensitive.
  • Anticipate the sequence (liner → base → bonding → restoration) so transfers are smooth and within working time.

Summary

Liners, bases, varnishes, and bonding systems work together to protect the pulp and secure the restoration. Liners and varnishes protect the deepest dentin; bases add insulation and bulk; bonding systems create the adhesive link for composite restorations. The choice depends largely on the depth of the preparation, the remaining dentin thickness, and the final restorative material. Accurate mixing, strict moisture control, and correct sequencing are the assistant's contributions to a long-lasting, comfortable restoration.
