An interview is your chance to show a dental office that you are not only trained but also dependable, friendly, and easy to work with — the qualities that make a great team member. Interviews feel less stressful when you practice. This guide helps you prepare: the common questions and how to answer them, what to bring, what to wear, smart questions to ask the office, and how to follow up. Read it once, then practice out loud (a mock interview with a classmate, friend, or instructor) so your answers feel natural on the day.

Learning objectives

  • Anticipate common dental-assistant interview questions and prepare clear answers.
  • Assemble what to bring and dress appropriately for an interview.
  • Ask thoughtful questions that show genuine interest in the office.
  • Follow up professionally after the interview.

Common questions and how to answer them

You cannot predict every question, but most fall into a few categories. Prepare a short, honest answer for each, and use specific examples from your training or externship.

"Tell me about yourself."

Give a 30–60-second summary: who you are, that you recently completed your dental-assistant training, a strength or two, and what you are looking for. Keep it professional and upbeat — this is your headline, not your life story.

"Why do you want to work here / be a dental assistant?"

Show that you researched the office and that you enjoy helping people. Mention something specific you noticed about the practice, and connect it to your goals.

"What are your strengths and weaknesses?"

Name a real strength relevant to the job (organization, calm under pressure, communication) with a quick example. For a weakness, name something genuine and say how you are working on it — never claim to have none.

Behavioral questions ("Tell me about a time...")

Use a simple story format: briefly describe the situation, what you did, and the result. For example, a time you handled a nervous patient, caught a mistake, or stayed organized during a busy day.

Skills and scope questions

Be honest about what you can do and what you are still learning. State your certifications (radiology, AHA BLS CPR) and emphasize that you are a fast, willing learner. A short list of practice questions

  • Tell me about yourself.
  • Why do you want to work at this office?
  • What made you choose dental assisting as a career?
  • What are your greatest strengths? What is an area you are improving?
  • Tell me about a time you dealt with a difficult or anxious patient or customer.
  • How do you handle a busy, fast-paced day?
  • What do you know about infection control and sterilization?
  • Are you comfortable taking radiographs? (Answer per your credentials.) [VERIFY: Amanda]
  • How do you work as part of a team?
  • Where do you see yourself in a couple of years?
  • Do you have any questions for us?

What to bring

  • Several printed copies of your resume on clean paper.
  • Copies of your certifications and AHA BLS CPR card.
  • A reference list with names and contact information (ask permission first).
  • A pen and a small notepad for notes and the questions you want to ask.
  • The office address, your interviewer's name, and the time — and plan to arrive 10–15 minutes early.

What to wear

Dress one notch above the everyday office dress code — clean, conservative, and professional. Business-casual is a safe choice: slacks or a simple skirt with a neat top, or clean professional scrubs if the office suggests them. Keep grooming tidy with neat hair, trimmed nails, minimal jewelry, and light or no fragrance (many patients and staff are sensitive to scents). A clean, put-together look signals that you will be careful and professional with patients.

Questions to ask the office

Always have two or three questions ready — it shows real interest. Good options include:

  • What does a typical day look like for an assistant here?
  • How is the team structured, and who would I work with most closely?
  • What does training or onboarding look like for a newer assistant?
  • What practice-management software do you use?
  • What do you enjoy most about working here?
  • What are the next steps in your hiring process?

Avoid leading with pay and time-off questions; let the office raise compensation, or save those for once an offer is on the table.

Follow up

Within about 24 hours, send a brief thank-you message (email is fine) to your interviewer. Thank them for their time, restate your interest in the role, and mention one specific thing you appreciated about the office or conversation. If they gave you a timeline and you have not heard back by then, one polite follow-up is appropriate. A timely, gracious thank-you keeps you top of mind and reinforces the professional impression you made in person.

Practice makes confident

The single best preparation is a real mock interview. Have a classmate, friend, or instructor ask you the practice questions above while you answer out loud — then ask for honest feedback on your answers, eye contact, and handshake. Run through it a few times until your responses feel natural rather than memorized. Walking in calm and prepared is exactly the professionalism dental offices are looking for.
