Your resume is usually the first thing a dental office sees, and you only need it to do one job: earn you an interview. Many new graduates worry that they have "nothing to put on it," but that is rarely true. Your training, externship, certifications, and transferable skills are exactly what offices want to see. This lesson shows you how to build a clean, honest dental-assistant resume even with little or no paid experience — which sections to include, what to put in each, how to format it, the mistakes to avoid, and a simple structure you can copy.

Learning objectives

  • List the standard sections of a dental-assistant resume.
  • Decide what to include when you have limited or no paid experience.
  • Use strong action verbs and clean formatting.
  • Recognize and avoid common resume mistakes.
  • Follow a simple sample structure to draft your own resume.

What goes on a dental-assistant resume

A focused, one-page resume is ideal for a new assistant. Include these sections, roughly in this order:

Contact information

Your full name, a professional phone number and email address, and your city. Use an email that is simply your name — not a nickname from years ago.

Professional summary or objective

Two or three sentences naming your goal and what you bring. Example: "Recent dental-assistant program graduate with hands-on externship experience, radiology certification, and current AHA BLS CPR. Seeking a chairside assistant role where I can support efficient, patient-centered care."

Certifications and credentials

List these prominently — they are some of your strongest selling points. Include your program completion, your radiology certification, and your AHA BLS CPR card with dates. Skills

A short, scannable list of clinical and soft skills: chairside assisting (four-handed dentistry), instrument sterilization and infection control, taking and processing dental radiographs (where credentialed), impressions/setup, patient communication, and any practice-management software you have used.

Education and training

Your dental-assistant program and graduation date, plus your high-school diploma or equivalent. New graduates put education near the top; once you have work experience, it moves lower.

Clinical experience / externship

This is where "no experience" turns into real experience. List your externship like a job: site name, dates, and bullet points describing what you did — the procedures you assisted with, the instruments you set up, the sterilization you performed, and how many patients or providers you supported.

Work history

Even unrelated jobs count. Retail, food service, or caregiving show reliability, teamwork, and customer service — all valued in a dental office. Keep it brief and highlight transferable skills.

References

You can write "References available upon request" or attach a separate reference page. Always ask people before listing them.

Use strong action verbs

Start each experience bullet with an action verb so your contributions sound active and concrete. Useful verbs for dental assistants include: assisted, prepared, sterilized, set up, scheduled, took (radiographs), educated, supported, maintained, documented, organized, and greeted. For example: "Sterilized and organized instruments to maintain a ready, compliant operatory," or "Assisted the dentist during restorative and hygiene procedures using four-handed technique."

Formatting

  • Keep it to one page with consistent fonts, sizes, and spacing.
  • Use clear section headings and short bullet points instead of long paragraphs.
  • Leave white space so it is easy to scan in a few seconds.
  • Save and send as a PDF so the layout does not shift.
  • Name the file professionally, such as "FirstName-LastName-Resume.pdf."

Common mistakes to avoid

  • Typos and grammar errors — proofread, then have someone else read it too.
  • Using one generic resume for every office instead of matching the job posting.
  • Listing duties as a plain list ("answered phones") instead of accomplishments with action verbs.
  • Leaving off certifications or burying them at the bottom.
  • Including personal details employers should not request (such as age, marital status, or a photo).
  • Overstating skills or experience — keep everything honest and verifiable.

A simple sample structure

You can copy this skeleton and fill in your details:

  • Name — phone, professional email, city
  • Professional Summary — 2–3 sentences
  • Certifications — program completion, radiology certification, AHA BLS CPR (with dates)
  • Skills — 6–10 clinical and soft skills
  • Education / Training — dental-assistant program + graduation date
  • Clinical Experience / Externship — site, dates, 3–5 action-verb bullets
  • Work History — brief, transferable skills highlighted
  • References — "available upon request"
