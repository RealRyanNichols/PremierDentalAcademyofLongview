How to pass the Texas dental jurisprudence exam

It's the law-and-ethics test every dental assistant should understand. Here's what it covers and a simple plan to be ready.

If you're working toward becoming a Registered Dental Assistant (RDA) in Texas, the jurisprudence assessment is one of the steps you'll want to take seriously. It isn't about drilling teeth or taking X-rays — it's about knowing the laws and ethics that govern how dental care is delivered in Texas. The good news is that this is one of the most learnable parts of your path. With a focused week of study, most students feel ready.

Below is a plain-English breakdown of what the assessment covers and a simple day-by-day plan you can follow.

What the jurisprudence assessment is

The jurisprudence assessment is an exam on Texas dental law and ethics, administered in connection with the Texas State Board of Dental Examiners (TSBDE). Its purpose is to make sure everyone working in a dental setting understands the rules that protect patients and keep the profession accountable.

Exam details such as the number of questions, the time allowed, and the score needed to pass can change over time, so this guide intentionally does not list those specifics. Always confirm the current format and requirements on the official TSBDE website before you sit for it. Treat the official site as your single source of truth.

What it covers

While the exact mix of topics can vary, the assessment generally focuses on these areas:

  • The Texas Dental Practice Act — the foundational state law that defines how dentistry is regulated.
  • TSBDE rules — the board's regulations that put the law into day-to-day practice.
  • Scope of practice — what an RDA may and may not do, and which tasks require additional certification or dentist supervision.
  • Infection control and recordkeeping rules — the standards that keep patients and staff safe and the requirements for maintaining accurate records.
  • Ethics and patient rights — professional conduct, confidentiality, and the responsibilities you owe to patients.

A simple 5-day study plan

You don't need months to prepare. A steady week, an hour or two a day, works well for most people:

  1. Day 1 — Read the Dental Practice Act overview. Get the big picture of how Texas regulates dentistry and why the law exists. Don't memorize yet; just build familiarity.
  2. Day 2 — Scope of practice. Focus on what an RDA is and isn't allowed to do. This is one of the most heavily tested areas, so give it extra attention.
  3. Day 3 — Infection control and records. Review safety standards and the rules for keeping accurate patient records.
  4. Day 4 — Ethics and patient rights. Study professional conduct, confidentiality, and patient protections.
  5. Day 5 — Practice questions and review. Take practice quizzes, then go back over any topics where you missed questions.

Study tips

  • Use official TSBDE materials as your primary source rather than relying on secondhand summaries.
  • Take practice quizzes to find your weak spots before exam day.
  • Spend the most time on scope-of-practice questions — they show up often and matter on the job.
  • Don't cram. Short, consistent sessions beat one long night of studying.

Where to find official materials

For current rules, the latest version of the Texas Dental Practice Act, and the most up-to-date exam information, go straight to the official TSBDE website. Because regulations and exam details are updated periodically, the official site is the only place you should trust for current specifics. If anything in another guide conflicts with the official site, the official site wins.
