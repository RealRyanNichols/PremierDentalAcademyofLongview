How to write a dental assistant job post that attracts good applicants

If your last posting brought in twenty applications and two worth interviewing, the posting itself is probably the problem. Here's a five-part blueprint East Texas offices can use to attract assistants who can actually work chairside — written from the school side, where we see what makes candidates say yes.

Why most postings fail

Most dental assistant postings are a wall of requirements and zero reasons to apply. Good candidates — the ones with options — skim for three things: what the day is actually like, whether the team seems sane, and whether the pay and hours are stated plainly. If your posting reads like a legal document, the candidates you want scroll past it and the ones with no options apply anyway. That's the mismatch.

The five-part blueprint (tap each part)

"Dental Assistant Needed" is invisible. "Chairside RDA for a 4-day-week family practice in Longview" tells a candidate in one line whether to keep reading. Lead with the one thing that makes your office a good place to work — schedule, team culture, mentorship, modern equipment — whatever is true.
Three or four sentences: what procedures you do most, how many patients a day, who they'd work beside, what the pace feels like. Candidates trained on real workflows can picture themselves in it — and self-select accurately, which saves your interview time.
List what's genuinely required (RDA registration or in progress, radiology/infection control, chairside competence) and separate it clearly from nice-to-haves. Ten bullet points of "requirements" that are really wishes scares off good newer candidates who would grow fast.
A real range and real hours beat "competitive pay, flexible schedule" every time. Postings with stated ranges consistently draw more qualified applicants — and it filters out mismatches before anyone's time is spent.
"Email your resume to..." with a named person beats a portal with twelve fields. Reply within two days — in this market, good assistants are gone by the weekend.

What to leave out

Skip the boilerplate paragraph about your mission statement, the demand for "5+ years experience" on an entry-level wage, and anything you wouldn't say to a candidate's face. One honest paragraph about a hard truth ("our Mondays are intense") builds more trust than a page of polish — and trust is what makes the good candidate pick you over the office down the road.

Writing for new graduates? Say so.

If you're open to a well-trained new grad, one line — "new RDAs with strong hands-on training welcome" — massively widens your pool. Our take on evaluating them: what to look for in a new RDA hire.
