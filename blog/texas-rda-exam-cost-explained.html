How much does the Texas RDA exam cost? (And what's included)

Here's the honest answer most schools dance around: there is no single "RDA exam fee." Becoming a Registered Dental Assistant in Texas is a handful of separate costs that people lump together — and knowing the pieces is the only way to budget without surprises. Let's lay them all out.

Why there's no single "exam fee"

When someone asks what the RDA exam costs, they're usually picturing one line item. In reality, the path to registration stacks a few things: the training course that prepares you, the exam(s) and state registration handled through the Texas State Board of Dental Examiners, a current BLS/CPR card, and the radiography requirement. Each is billed by a different party, which is exactly why the total feels fuzzy. Break it into pieces and it gets clear fast.

The pieces of the bill

1 · The training course

This is the biggest and most controllable number. At Premier Dental Academy, our In-Person program is $3,000 (or $3,500 on a payment plan with $500 down), and the fully online program is on sale at $397 right now (regularly $997). That tuition covers your instruction and hands-on training — the foundation everything else sits on.

2 · The exam(s) & state registration

Fees for the required exam(s) and your registration with the state are set by the TSBDE and its testing vendor — not by any school. Because those numbers change, we don't quote them here; check the official site below for the current amounts.

3 · BLS / CPR certification

A current Basic Life Support card is standard for clinical roles. It's a modest cost through the American Heart Association or a local provider, and it's renewed on a cycle — budget for it separately.

4 · Radiography

Taking dental x-rays in Texas has its own requirement. Read how dental radiology certification works in Texas so you know how it fits your timeline and budget.

Where to find the official numbers

For anything set by the state — exam fees, registration fees, retake fees, renewal cycles — go straight to the board: tsbde.texas.gov. It's the one source that's always current, and it's free to read. If a school quotes you an all-in "exam price," ask them to itemize it against that list — a straight answer is a good sign; a dodge is a red flag.

Funding can cover a lot of it

Here's the part that surprises people: for many East Texans, a big chunk of this comes down to $0 out of pocket. Training that leads to a real job often qualifies for WIOA funding through Workforce Solutions East Texas, and veterans may be able to use GI Bill benefits. We accept all three. Walk through our free funding finder to see which doors are open to you, and read grants & financial aid for Texas dental assistants for the full picture. Funding is applied for directly through those agencies — we'll help you get the paperwork right.

Compare apples to apples.

When you price schools, add every piece — course + exam + BLS + radiography — for each option. A "cheap" course that leaves you to sort out the rest alone can end up costing more in money and months than one that walks you through the whole path.

Two traps to watch for

The first is the "one low price" pitch. A course advertised for a rock-bottom number can look like a steal until you realize the exam, BLS, and radiography are all left for you to arrange and pay for on your own. That's not necessarily dishonest — but it means the sticker price isn't the real price. Always total every piece before you compare schools.

The second is confusing a payment plan with a discount. A plan (like our $3,500 In-Person option with $500 down) changes when you pay, not the underlying value of what's included — it exists to make the cost manageable month to month, and the certificate is issued once tuition is paid in full.
