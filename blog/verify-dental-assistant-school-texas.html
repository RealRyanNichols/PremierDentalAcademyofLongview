How to verify a dental assistant school is legit in Texas

You don't have to take anyone's word for it — including ours. Texas gives you public tools to check a school before you spend a dollar. Here's exactly how to use them, what the official agencies actually do, and how to match a program's curriculum to the real requirements for working as a dental assistant.

"Is this school real?" is a smart question, and there's no shame in asking it. The good news: in Texas, the answer is a matter of public record. You just need to know which two agencies to look at and what each one is responsible for.

Two agencies, two different jobs

People mix these up all the time, so let's separate them clearly. The Texas Workforce Commission (TWC) licenses career schools — it regulates the business that trains you. The Texas State Board of Dental Examiners (TSBDE) governs the dental assistant credential itself — the registrations and certificates you earn to legally perform certain duties, like taking x-rays. One checks the school; the other sets the rules for you. A legitimate program lines up with both.

Step 1: Look up the school's TWC career-school license

A career school operating in Texas should hold a TWC license, and TWC publishes a searchable directory of licensed schools. Go to twc.texas.gov, find the Career Schools and Colleges directory, and search the school's name. You should find a matching record and a license number. For us, that number is TWC Career School License #S5316 — you can look it up and see it for yourself. If a school can't give you a number, or the number doesn't match a real record, stop there.

Step 2: Learn what the TSBDE requires of you

To work as a Registered Dental Assistant in Texas, the state — not the school — sets the requirements, which generally include training in areas like radiology, jurisprudence, and infection control, plus registering with the board. The authoritative source is the board itself: tsbde.texas.gov. Read the requirements straight from them, and treat any school's summary (including ours) as a starting point, not the final word. We walk through the registration path in plain English in our Texas RDA registration guide.

Words that trip people up

Accredited, licensed, and registered don't mean the same thing — and a school can be perfectly legitimate without being "accredited" in the college sense. We untangle all three terms in this post so nobody can use the vocabulary to confuse you.

Step 3: Match the curriculum to the requirements

Once you know what the state expects, look at whether the program actually teaches it. Does the course cover radiology, infection control, and the clinical skills you'll use chairside? Is there real hands-on time, or only videos? You're checking for alignment: a legit school's curriculum should map cleanly onto the things the board and real offices care about. If you're comparing programs, bring the questions in this checklist to each one.

Step 4: Confirm the things you can see with your own eyes

The official records matter, but so do the ordinary signs of a real business: a physical address you can drive to, a price stated openly, a way to reach a human, and the option to visit. Being honest about what we are — and aren't

Since this is a trust article, here's the plain truth: Premier Dental Academy is a TWC-licensed career school. We are not the state board, and we can't register you as an RDA — you do that with the TSBDE, and you're responsible for completing the state's steps. We don't guarantee licensure or a job; no honest school can. What we can promise is that everything we ask you to verify above, you can verify. That's the standard we'd hold any school to, so it's the one we hold ourselves to.

What a license does — and doesn't — promise

Verifying a school protects you from the worst outcomes: a fly-by-night "program" that takes your money and disappears, or one that can't legally operate at all. That protection is real and worth ten minutes of your time. But be clear-eyed about what it means. A TWC license says a school met the state's requirements to run as a career school; it does not, by itself, guarantee you'll pass a board exam, land a job, or enjoy the experience. Those depend on the teaching, the hands-on hours, and the work you put in. So do both halves of the homework: confirm the paperwork, then judge the substance — visit, sit in on a class if you can, and ask graduates what the room was actually like.

One more honest note: licensing and accreditation aren't the same thing, and neither one is a stand-in for fit. A small licensed school with real chairside time may serve you far better than a bigger name that keeps you behind a screen. Verify first, then choose the place that will actually get your hands ready.
